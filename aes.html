AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)

Overview

AES is a symmetric block cipher chosen by the U.S. government to protect classified information. It was established by the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in 2001.

Working

AES operates on a 4×4 column-major order matrix of bytes, termed the state. The key size used for an AES cipher specifies the number of repetitions of transformation rounds: 10 rounds for 128-bit keys, 12 rounds for 192-bit keys, and 14 rounds for 256-bit keys.

Application

AES is widely used for secure communication in various applications, including wireless security, file encryption, and secure communications over the internet (HTTPS).

Time Complexity: O(M⋅Nb⋅Nr) ≈ O(1) The total time complexity for encrypting/decrypting a message of M blocks is the product of the time complexity per block and the number of blocks, which are constants- standard number of rounds are 10 (128 bits), 12 (192 bits) or 14 (256 bits) depending on the key size.

Space Complexity: O(Nk⋅(Nr+1)+Nb2) ≈ O(1) Includes both the expanded key storage and the state storage.

Example

Plaintext: "Hello, World!"

Key: "ThisIsASecretKey"
